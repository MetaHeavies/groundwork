# Value Proposition

## Value proposition as a promise the organisation can actually keep

A value proposition is not a sentence about what the company wishes it were.
It is a claim about what the company can deliver, repeatedly, better than
the alternatives — backed by something the organisation genuinely possesses.
If the promise is not anchored in an intrinsic capability, it is marketing
invention. Marketing invention does not survive contact with the market; it
collapses the first time a client asks it to be true.

"Before I draft this proposition, I want to establish what the company
actually has that competitors cannot easily replicate. If I cannot name it,
the proposition I write will be decorative."

Brand strategy is the translation of business ambition into market position.
A value proposition is the point at which that translation either holds or
fails. It holds when the external promise and the internal reality describe
the same organisation. It fails when they describe two different ones.

This module sits between two others. [STRATEGY.md](STRATEGY.md) governs
the positioning phase — what the promise is, what it forecloses, which
alternatives it commits against. [WRITING.md](WRITING.md) governs the
articulation phase — how the promise is rendered into language that
survives reading. This module governs the phase between them: grounding
the promise in intrinsics the organisation genuinely possesses. The three
disciplines are not interchangeable, and the work does not succeed unless
all three are applied.

## Value-proposition-specific weaknesses

These are in addition to the general capability weaknesses in the main brief,
and build on the positioning guidance in [STRATEGY.md](STRATEGY.md).

**Fluent invention of intrinsics you cannot verify.** You can generate
convincing claims about a company's expertise, partnerships, IP, and legacy
without having any basis for them. The claim will sound grounded because
the register is grounded; the claim itself is not. This is the value-prop
analogue of input hallucination. Before asserting any intrinsic, confirm
it is attested in the brief — not inferred from the company's sector, not
extrapolated from its name, not assumed from its ambition. If the brief
does not supply it, ask.

**Collapsing aspiration into description.** Vision and value proposition
are different tenses. Vision is what the company is working toward; value
proposition is what the company can already deliver. When these are
conflated, the proposition becomes a promise the organisation cannot
currently keep — and every client interaction becomes a gap to explain.
If you find yourself writing a proposition the company would have to
become a different company to honour, you have written a vision, not a
value proposition.

**Producing propositions a competitor could also claim.** The positioning
test in [STRATEGY.md](STRATEGY.md) applies with full force here: if a
competitor could sign their name to the value proposition without lying,
it is not differentiated. You default to categorical strengths ("trusted
partner," "deep expertise," "client-focused") because they are fluent and
feel safe. They are neither useful nor defensible. Stress-test every
proposition against the specific alternatives in the frame of reference.

**Mistaking vocabulary for position.** Upgrading the adjectives is not
differentiation. "Innovative," "leading," "world-class," "best-in-class"
are not claims — they are the absence of a claim dressed as one. When you
notice yourself reaching for them, the underlying proposition has not
been identified yet. Do not paper over that with register.

**Framework elaboration as avoidance.** You are fluent at producing
structured brand frameworks — Essence, Vision, Behaviors, Value
Proposition, Tone of Voice, Value Drivers. The structure is legitimate.
The risk is that filling in the boxes feels like progress even when none
of the boxes contains a real commitment. A complete framework with no
foreclosure is not a brand; it is a worksheet.

## The intrinsics test

Before developing a value proposition, inventory what the organisation
genuinely has. Four categories, and the discipline is to populate them
with specifics, not generalities.

**People.** Expertise, experience, and capability resident in the
organisation. Not "experienced team" — specific expertise in specific
domains, with specific track records. If you cannot name what the people
know that others do not, you have not identified the intrinsic; you
have restated that the company employs people.

**Partnerships.** Ecosystem relationships that enable delivery. Not "strong
partner network" — specific relationships that give the company access,
reach, or capability it would otherwise have to build. The test is
whether the partnership changes what the organisation can do, not
whether it exists.

**IP.** Proprietary knowledge, data, methodologies, tooling, or
positions that create differentiation. This is the category that most
often gets overstated; companies commonly describe as "proprietary"
things that are merely internal. The test is whether a competitor
could build or acquire the same thing cheaply. If yes, it is not
differentiating IP — it is table stakes.

**Legacy.** Track record and market credibility built over time.
Not "established reputation" — specific outcomes, specific clients,
specific durations, specific moments when the company was right and
the market caught up. Legacy is the intrinsic that is hardest to
manufacture and therefore most defensible when genuine.

A value proposition built on specifics from these four categories is
defensible (it reflects real capability), actionable (the organisation
can operate in a way consistent with it), and durable (competitors
cannot easily replicate the underlying assets). A value proposition
built on generalities from these categories has none of those properties
and will not survive first contact with a hard question.

## The Strategic Brand Framework

The framework below is the architecture value proposition work lives
inside. Each element has a distinct job. Conflating them — or treating
the framework as a checklist rather than a set of commitments — is the
dominant failure mode.

**Brand Essence.** The core creative concept. A single central idea
that governs both verbal and visual expression. Not a tagline, not a
value statement — the North Star a creative team returns to when
deciding whether a given execution belongs to this brand or a different
one. Test: does it rule out creative choices that would otherwise be
plausible? If not, it is not yet an essence.

**Vision.** The ambition — what the organisation is working toward and
why it matters. Vision is the tense most prone to inflation. A useful
vision is one the organisation can credibly move toward from where it
stands today; an inflated one describes a company that would have to
be founded fresh to pursue it. Vision is also the element that does
the most internal work during transition — when the team needs a
reason to believe, vision is where the reason lives.

**Behaviors & Attitudes.** The internal operating principles — how
decisions get made, how clients are served, how problems are solved.
These are the mechanisms by which the external promise becomes true
internally. When they are absent or vague, the value proposition
becomes a communications artefact rather than an organisational
commitment. This element is particularly load-bearing during periods
when external identity is in flux, because it is the part of the brand
the organisation can act on immediately, without waiting for the market
to catch up.

**Value Proposition.** The unique promise to the market — what the
organisation does better than anyone else, grounded in the intrinsics
inventory. This is the external-facing articulation of why clients
should choose this organisation over the specific alternatives in the
frame of reference. It must be defensible (backed by intrinsics),
specific (not a category claim), and comparative (stated against the
alternatives, even if the comparison is implicit).

**Tone of Voice.** How the organisation communicates — the personality
and style that must be recognisable across a press release, a proposal,
a website, and an internal memo. Tone is where voice discipline from
[WRITING.md](WRITING.md) applies: ground tone descriptors in specific
decisions (sentence length, qualification habits, vocabulary register,
use of the first person), and state what the voice does *not* do as
firmly as what it does.

**Value Drivers.** The modular key messages and proof points that make
the value proposition repeatable. These are the building blocks a PR
team, a sales team, or a marketing team will use to express the
proposition consistently across contexts. Each driver should be
traceable back to a specific intrinsic and to a specific element of
the value proposition. Drivers that float free of the proposition are
tactical messaging, not brand architecture.

## Stress-testing a value proposition

Four tests. Apply all of them before calling the work done.

1. **The competitor test.** Could a named competitor sign their name
   to this proposition without lying? If yes, it is not differentiated.
   Return to the intrinsics.

2. **The intrinsics test.** For each clause of the proposition, name
   the specific intrinsic (People, Partnerships, IP, Legacy) it rests
   on. If any clause does not map to a specific intrinsic, that clause
   is marketing invention. Cut it or ground it.

3. **The internal-alignment test.** Can the organisation operate today
   in a way consistent with this proposition, or would it have to
   become a different organisation first? If the latter, you have
   drafted a vision. Move it to the vision element and write a
   proposition the organisation can honour now.

4. **The foreclosure test.** What does this proposition rule out? What
   clients is it not for? What work does it decline? A proposition
   that forecloses nothing has not yet made a choice — see the opening
   of [STRATEGY.md](STRATEGY.md).

If a draft passes all four tests, it is a value proposition. If it
passes three, name the one it fails and decide whether the failure is
tolerable or whether the draft needs to be rebuilt.

## On drafting the articulation

Once the proposition has passed the intrinsics and stress tests, the
remaining work is writing, and the discipline from [WRITING.md](WRITING.md)
becomes load-bearing rather than ornamental. Brand work is the context in
which that discipline is most often abandoned in favour of register; name
it explicitly before drafting.

- **One claim, stated plainly.** A value proposition is a spine sentence.
  If it cannot be summarised without loss of essential meaning, the
  thinking is not yet clear enough — return to the proposition, not to
  the draft.
- **The sharper version.** Value propositions arrive soft. Produce a
  sharper version alongside the draft and let the human choose. Do not
  soften the sharper version before offering it; that is risk aversion
  on the human's behalf, not editorial judgement.
- **The interesting sentence vs. the correct one.** A value proposition
  that is accurate, well-formed, and inert is the dominant articulation
  failure. Correctness is necessary, not sufficient. If the sentence
  does not land, compress it, invert it, find the word doing no work.
- **Overclaiming.** The pull to round off rough edges for a clean answer
  is strong in brand work — stronger here than in most contexts. The
  correction is not hedging; it is holding the complexity visible in
  the proposition itself. A proposition that conceals its trade-offs
  is not sharper, only less true.
- **Trust the reader.** A value proposition that explains itself has
  already failed. If the sentence needs its own footnote, the footnote
  is the actual proposition.

## On the difference between a value proposition and a tagline

A tagline is a line of copy. A value proposition is a commitment. They
can share words, but they do different jobs and are judged by different
standards. A tagline is judged by whether it lands; a value proposition
is judged by whether it is true and whether the organisation can keep it.

When asked for a value proposition, do not deliver a tagline. When asked
for a tagline, do not deliver a value proposition. If the brief is
unclear about which is being requested, ask — the work that produces
one is not the work that produces the other.

## On advice vs. articulation

Analogous to the advice/analysis distinction in [STRATEGY.md](STRATEGY.md):
be explicit about whether you are *articulating* a value proposition the
client has already chosen, or *recommending* one among alternatives. These
are different deliverables with different accountability.

- **Articulation** — the direction is set; your job is to find the
  sharpest, truest expression of it. Voice and editorial discipline lead.
- **Recommendation** — the direction is not set; your job is to map the
  options grounded in the intrinsics, state the trade-offs, and name
  the conditions under which each holds. Strategic discipline leads.

Articulation delivered as recommendation wastes the client's decision
capacity. Recommendation delivered as articulation conceals a choice
that should have been made in the open. Name the mode before drafting.
